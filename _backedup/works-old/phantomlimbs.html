Phantom Limbs
A Memorial Painting Cycle and Museum Installation (2001-2011)

Phantom Limbs is an installation of nine framed paintings and related visual materials produced between October 2001 and July 2011. It is presented as an intervention within institutions and museums that house Western canonical paintings and contemporary art. The paintings are also exhibited individually or in smaller groups, and they are each 36x48 inches (unframed). Each work in this series corresponds to a place in the world where the New York Twin Towers are fictionally reconstructed as an international memorial. Some of these places are contested territories or relevant sites for international affairs between 2001 and 2011. Other sites correspond to cities where the Twin Towers have actually been recreated by light beams to commemorate September 11, 2001. Each painting has different stylistic, iconographic, and technical characteristics that bring various layers of complexity to the independent paintings and the installation as a whole. While the paintings are the central component, the work includes sketches, writings, and visual materials for optional display.

The overall series represents new WTC buildings in Kabul, Budapest, Baghdad, Paris, Guant√°namo Bay, Liverpool, Darfur Region refugee camps, Medellin, Montevideo, New Orleans, Panmunjum DMZ (North-South Korea), and Gaza City.

Twin Towers Go Global (2006-2012)

An Internet Memorial Using New and Old Social Media


Twin Towers Go Global is a new media work that complements the Phantom Limbs series and shares much of the same research material. Also based on the premise that the New York Twin Towers are being reconstructed around the world as living memorials, the project interweaves this fictional framework with ten years of research and factual information through a website, social networks, news stories, an international competition, and collaborations with existing organizations, such as AND AND AND / Documenta 13. The difference in media often leads viewers to infer that the website, architectural renderings, and social media of TTGG are factual, while the paintings from Phantom Limbs are fictional. The assumptions associated with specific media and their social circuits are part of the content of these works, and they constitute a key reason for the broad range of media.
